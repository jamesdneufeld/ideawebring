Why Tiny Web Badges Matter

This page is an invitation into a different way of thinking about the web. Not because badges are technically important but because they point to a version of the internet where people built relationships directly into their pages.

What are 88×31 badges?

88×31 badges are small images people used to place on personal websites. They usually acted as identity markers, friendship signals, affiliation symbols, links to other websites, and tiny declarations of "I exist here, and I belong to this."

They are intentionally small. Not because they are insignificant, but because they were meant to be shared, not optimized. You don't "scroll past" a badge. You notice it or you don't. That's part of its logic.

History / Culture

In the early web — GeoCities pages, personal homepages, indie hosting communities — websites weren't platforms, they were rooms. People built them slowly and by hand, one HTML file at a time. Those rooms were decorated with animated GIFs, visitor counters, tiled backgrounds, hand-made icons, and 88×31 badges. Badges were a kind of public stitching. One site linking to another visually, not just through text. A network you could see, not just click through.

Why They Matter

A badge is not just a graphic. It is a relationship. It quietly says: I acknowledge this other site. I trust it enough to display it. I am part of something with it. It is a direct choice with no algorithm or platform decisions. Modern platforms remove technical friction by hiding how things work. Everything becomes seamless, but also centralized and invisible. Badges do the opposite. They make the network visible again.

Connection, Identity, and Navigation

Badges tend to fall into two overlapping categories. Identity badges say: "This is who I am. This is what I'm part of. This is my aesthetic, community, or belief space." Navigation badges say: "Go here next. You might like this site. This is part of the path I want to show you." Both are handmade and assume the web is something you move through socially, not just consume.

Badge Swaps

A badge swap is one of the simplest agreements on the web: "I will display your badge if you display mine." Over time, this creates a network that is lived in.

Why This Matters for Students

Most modern web experiences teach you that websites are endpoints. You arrive. You consume. You leave. Badges suggest websites are nodes and nodes connect to other nodes. A personal page becomes part of a network.

Experimentation is the Point

Badges are meant to be weird, inconsistent, slightly nostalgic, sometimes ugly, sometimes animated, sometimes confusing. It is permission to treat the web as a creative space again, not just a professional one.

Invitation

Make a badge. Or don't. Make it serious. Or completely wrong. Make one for yourself. Make one for a friend. Make one that only makes sense inside your own small corner of the web. Then display it and see what happens when it connects to something else.

Closing Idea

This is what the IDEA Web Ring is trying to preserve: not just websites, but relationships between them. Visible, handmade, and intentional. Because when those connections are visible, the web stops feeling like a feed and starts feeling like a place again.
